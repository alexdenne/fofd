Guest Speaker

Dominic North

Ranger, Happy Valley

A Guided Tour of Happy Valley

Dominic North provided an in-depth look at the ecology, management and conservation of Happy Valley.

History and Geology

The 250 acre site was bought in 1937 by Coulsdon & Purley UDC under the Green Belt Act. The area includes:

  • Devilsden Wood
  • Glebelands (given by Caterham & Warlingham UDC)
  • Parson Pightle Estate (added later)

The name 'Happy Valley' was given in 1970. The steep-sided dry valley was formed during the last Ice Age and is mainly chalk with a band of clay and flint soil on the western slope.

Conservation Designations

  • Site of Special Scientific Interest (SSSI) - Important for wildlife
  • Site of Metropolitan Importance for Nature Conservation (SINC)
  • Forms a link between Farthing Downs (north) and Coulsdon Common (east), both owned by the City of London

Post-WWII Challenges

Up to 80% of chalk grassland was lost due to:

  • Lack of grazing
  • Myxomatosis reducing rabbit population
  • Land leased to farmer for haymaking which was not carried out
  • Resulting scrub invasion

Recovery: When management returned to Croydon Council in 1966, Surrey Wildlife Trust advised on scrub clearance and grazing project (1968/69) on Southern slopes, bringing improvements.

Rare and Beautiful Plant Species

Orchids

  • Bee Orchid - Self-pollinating, flowers June-July
  • Pyramidal Orchid - Bright pink pyramidal flowers
  • Fly Orchid - Very vulnerable, on IUCN Red List, typically 10-20 found annually
  • Man Orchid - Endangered, humanoid-shaped flowers, grows to about a foot high

Other Rare Plants

  • Round Headed Rampion - Nationally scarce
  • Woolly Thistle - Now rare in London
  • Greater Knapweed - Attractive to butterflies
  • Greater Yellow Rattle - Nationally rare (only 6 sites in Britain), now abundant on Happy Valley and Farthing Downs

Wildlife Success

  • Insects: Around 800 species benefit from increased wildflowers
  • Butterflies: Around 30 species recorded in annual transects, including new species like Silver Washed Fritillary
  • Burnett Moth: Day-flying moth frequently seen
  • Roman Snail: Schedule 5 protected species found on both Happy Valley and Farthing Downs

Grassland Management

Grazing Programme (reintroduced 2002)

More effective than tractor cutting, encourages wildlife diversity, and protects ant hills.

Sheep Breeds Used

  • Hardwicks - Saved by Beatrix Potter, eat thorny scrub
  • Jacobs Sheep - Hardy breed, eat rough scrub, chase off dogs to protect flock
  • Speckled Face Beulah's - Hardy Welsh mountain sheep

Management: 120 sheep weeks needed per hectare per year (varies with conditions, public use requirements, and rights of way)

Volunteer Support: Shepherds regularly check flocks for fly strike and other problems. Volunteers also help with ragwort pulling (burnt on site).

Haymaking: Valley fields cut by local farmer who keeps the hay.

Devilsden Wood - Ancient Semi-Natural Woodland

The woodland straddles both chalk & flint and clay & flint soils, dating back many hundreds of years.

Ancient Woodland Indicators

  • Bluebells
  • Sweet Woodruff
  • Bird's Nest Orchid
  • Abundant Dog's Mercury

Coppicing: 15-year rotational basis (Neolithic practice). Varying heights support different wildlife, particularly Dormouse (protected species). Coppicing creates mosaic of different interconnected conditions.

Dormouse Monitoring: 50 dormouse boxes checked annually, data forwarded to National Dormouse Monitoring Program.

Conservation Conclusion

Dominic concluded by saying that as well as being a pretty place to walk, there is much conservation work being carried out on Happy Valley to protect and encourage wildlife - and he believes he has one of the best jobs in Croydon Council.
