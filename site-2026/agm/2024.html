Panel Discussion

Housing, Green Belt, Brownfield, Grey Field

A Discussion on Government Policy

Speakers:

  • Richard Biggs - Leader of Reigate and Bansted Council, Parliamentary Secretary for East Surrey MP Claire Coutinho
  • Richard Knox-Johnson - Chair of the London Green Belt Council
  • Tom Oliver - Conservation Officer for the City of London's Coulsdon Commons

A Discussion on Government Policy

Panel discussion examining the complex challenges of government housing targets, Green Belt protection, Grey Belt designation, and the case for prioritizing brownfield development. Speakers explored the pressures facing local planning authorities, the vulnerabilities of London's Green Belt, and the economic and environmental benefits of preserving green spaces.

Richard Biggs: Reigate and Banstead Perspective

Council Commitment: Passionate about open spaces but pressure to provide homes creates difficult balance.

Grey Belt Designation

Concerns:

  • Description too loose
  • National policy on developable land needed
  • Difficulty: any Grey Belt development needs 50% affordable homes

Housing Targets

Government Policy Changes:

  • Reigate and Banstead requirement almost doubled: from 644 to 1,264 homes/year
  • Local plan requirement remains 460/annum until 2027
  • Government proposals require 20% more development land (most Green Belt)

Challenge: Not sure how targets will be met. Build high is one answer (Redhill Station tower blocks: 18 stories). Question: whose need met? How to provide affordable houses for families?

Solutions Needed

  • More comprehensive approach
  • County allocated housing targets by constituency
  • More consultation
  • Neighbourhood Plans - not just about stopping building, but where development can take place

Richard Knox-Johnson: London Green Belt Council

LGBC Background:

  • Formed 1955
  • Growing membership: 110 organizations in London Metropolitan Green Belt

Housing Need Calculation

Method like "Duckworth Lewis cricket score calculator" - no one challenges, no one understands.

New Two-Step Method:

  1. Baseline: Projected household growth for next 10 years
  2. Affordability adjustment: Upward adjustment where house prices more than 4x higher than earnings
  3. Cap: Increase capped at 40% of baseline
  4. Urban uplift: For LPAs containing largest proportion of population in one of England's 20 largest cities, increase by 35%

London Green Belt Vulnerability

Reasons:

  • 15 London Boroughs with Green Belt land
  • 47 Local Planning Authorities
  • 62 planning districts
  • Results in piecemeal planning and development
  • No overall strategy or vision

Protection Actions Needed

  • Formation of London Metropolitan Green Belt Authority
  • Spatial planning
  • Geographic strategy
  • More consistency between Local Authorities
  • National body for referral of Green Belt development applications

Benefits of Green Belt Land

Economic:

  • Flood prevention alone saves trillions
  • Food production: ¬£2M/annum turnover from people buying directly from farmers in London

Environmental:

  • Carbon sequestration

Health and Wellbeing:

  • Professor Jules Pretty (Essex University): 50% of population suffer stress
  • Green Belt provides beneficial recreation, family time

Grey Belt Designation Issues

Without Succinct Definition:

  • Could refer to garages/car parks within Green Belt
  • Could result in loss of thousands of hectares
  • Cumulative effects on openness and attractiveness
  • Loss of nature and biodiversity

Brownfield Sites Should Be First Consideration

Advantages:

  • Close to town centres
  • Transport and infrastructure in place
  • Appeals to younger people
  • Affordable housing works
  • Economic advantages: save infrastructure costs, regenerate urban areas

Current Status:

  • CPRE research: London Borough of Enfield has enough brownfield sites for 37,000 homes
  • CPRE reveals: enough brownfield sites for 1M homes in London
  • Currently 1M homes with planning permission not being built

Tom Oliver: City of London's Open Spaces History

1850s-60s: Manorial Landscape

  • Wasteland used by local people for grazing
  • Railway building pushed London further out - land needed for development
  • Wasteland under pressure from enclosure threat
  • Loss of 'rights of common' enjoyed by commoners

Corporation of London (Open Spaces) Act 1878

  • Enables City to manage Commons in perpetuity
  • Meaning: no building

Maintained Since 1883

  • Two Sites of Special Scientific Interest (SSSI)
  • Habitat to 11 species of chalk grassland flowers

Recent Acquisitions

New Hill:

  • Building along Chaldon Way stopped 1940
  • Bought by City with donations from local residents

Woodplace Farm Fields (2005):

  • Bought by City
  • Donations from local residents

Lesson: Often saving open spaces requires co-operation between Local Authorities and residents.

Chairman's Summary

The Chair thanked all speakers for detailed and interesting presentations explaining dangers and pitfalls facing London Green Belt future - clearly appreciated and valued by those present.
