Welcome to the Happy Valley and Farthing Downs Nature Trail

Discover the fascinating wildlife and history of this beautiful area of countryside with our interactive walk.

The trail can be walked in one go or as two sections. The Farthing Downs section (posts 1 to 10) is reasonably flat and takes roughly 1¬Ω hours. The Happy Valley section (posts 11 to 29) includes some steep hills and steps and takes roughly 2 hours to walk.

How to follow the trail:

  1. The route is shown by red discs mounted on wooden posts.
  2. At each numbered post, tap on the appropriate number on the nature trail home page to discover the features at each location.
  3. Using the back arrow on your phone or browser should return you to the nature trail home page at each stop. Alternatively you can scan the QR code at each post to return to the home page.
  4. To help you follow the trail, a map and directions are given below the text for each post.

GPS Navigation

You can also follow the trail using your smartphone's GPS, by using one of two free apps: Viewranger or TiCL.

Wildlife spotter game

You may like to try to spot some of the wildlife shown in this guide. You score points for each plant or animal seen and add up your score at the end of the trail. Rarer plants & animals score more points.

Directions

To walk the Farthing Downs section: Go along the top of the Downs towards the wooden fence, keeping the road to your right. Post 2 is next to the red dog bin, near to the cattle grid. There are several visitor information boards in this area which give more details of the history and wildlife of Farthing Downs and the surrounding area.

To walk the Happy Valley section: Cross over the road, heading back towards the cottages. Look out for an arrowed post pointing through the trees on your left to post 11.

2

Green Belt Land

Learn about the preservation of this beautiful landscape and the conservation efforts that saved it from development.

Facing across the road, you have a commanding view of New Hill. These 34 hectares were bought by the City of London Corporation in 2002 to preserve the area for recreation and wildlife. Looking to the right you will see Happy Valley. In 1937 this area was described as 'one of the most beautiful valleys in the district' and just over 100 hectares of land were bought by the local authority for its preservation.

New Hill had been allocated for housing in the 1940's, but was saved from development when the area was designated as green belt land.

Development History

The area still belonged to a property developer until 2002. During the intervening years, the land had been used for agriculture and horse grazing, but had eventually fallen into neglect. Young trees and scrub colonised the fields, leading to the loss of many of the open wild flower meadows.

Conservation Efforts

The grassland that survived was very rich in places, with 8 species of orchid and other rarities being recorded. Some of the larger areas of scrub have since been cleared and livestock grazing has been introduced to keep an open aspect.

Directions

Now pass through the gate and turn left down the diagonal path, looking out for a directional marker to take you to post 3.

3

Iron Age Field System

Discover the ancient field boundaries and farming methods from over 2000 years ago.

The bank near to the path that you have been walking on dates back to the Iron Age, and was originally part of an ancient track or droveway, produced by the people who farmed Farthing Downs over 2000 years ago.

Field Boundaries

Other banks found on Farthing Downs are Iron Age field boundaries. The fields were usually less than 2 acres (0.8 hectares) in size, and rectangular in shape, running down both slopes from the track which ran roughly along the summit.

The banks were formed by the downward movement of soil following ploughing. This hollowed out some of the material on the slope leaving a 'negative lynchet' and accumulated soil at the foot of each field, building up a 'positive lynchet'. If you drove to the start of the trail along the top of Farthing Downs you may have noticed the bumps in the road which are caused by these lynchets.

Ancient Agriculture

The thin soils found over the chalk are not very fertile, but the Iron Age people were able to clear the natural vegetation and cultivate these areas using primitive ploughs - merely a crooked piece of wood drawn through the soil by a pair of oxen guided by a ploughman.

Later History

The Romano-British settlers of the 1st and 2nd centuries A.D. used the top of the Downs for growing wheat. Their successors, the Saxons, farmed the valley bottoms where, with their improved plough, they could cultivate the deeper, richer soils. The top of the Downs was then abandoned and used only as a cemetery. Since that time Farthing Downs received little or no cultivation and so the Iron Age field lines can still be seen.

Woodplace Farm Fields

The path to post 4 veers off to the left and after a short distance crosses a positive lynchet. Look for a directional marker.

Pause at this post to admire the view across the valley to Woodplace Farm Fields. These two fields were saved from the threat of development in 2004 after a public appeal led by local residents raised enough money to assist the City of London in purchasing the land.

Directions

Head directly down the slope to the hedgerow and post 4.
