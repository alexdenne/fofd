History & Archaeology

6,000 years of human history on the Downs

Scheduled Ancient Monument

Farthing Downs is protected by law as a site of national archaeological importance, spanning from the Neolithic period (4000 BC) through to the Medieval era (1066 AD).

Living in Coulsdon 2000 Years Ago

The map of Ancient Britain published in 2010 cites Farthing Downs as a site of 'early mediaeval' funerary importance. Within a twenty-mile radius, only ten other places significant in ancient history receive such recognition - making this landscape truly exceptional.

The Bigger Picture

In early times, settlers avoided lowlands in favour of higher ground above the tree-line, and only settled below where shallow soils could be worked with simple implements. It's only because Anglo-Saxon, Jute, Celtic-speaking Belgae, and Scandinavian migrants favoured river valley bottoms - where oxen-yoked ploughs could cope with heavier soils - that lowland England became more populated.

Contrast this agrarian way of life with more industrial living across the west of Upland Britain, where for two centuries tin had already been traded across Europe via St. Michael's Mount. In the Forest of Dean, iron had long been worked into currency bars, coins of bronze were forged, and even gold was minted. With such development came hill forts like Maiden Castle and Bredon Hill to give communities protection against raiders - all well before Farthing Downs saw its elementary field system evolve.

Archaeological Investigations

1770

One burial mound opened up at the northern cluster - the first documented excavation on the Downs.

1871

J Wickham Flower opened up sixteen burial mounds across the site, significantly expanding our understanding of Saxon burials.

1944

Hope-Taylor maps the droveway and the whole field system - groundbreaking work that revealed the Iron Age agricultural landscape.

1948/9

Hope-Taylor conducts more detailed mapping of burial mounds and surrounding features.

2005/6

Extensive reassessment of previous archaeological work with follow-up investigations. Ground near the Millennium Cairn explored.

2015-2022

Major excavations at Lion Green Road Car Park uncover numerous Roman and Anglo-Saxon burials, transforming our understanding of the area. Bodies of adults, a dog, Saxon beakers, and over 18 burials discovered. The Museum of London's report runs to 54 pages with extensive illustrations.

Archaeological Discoveries by Period

4000 BC - 2300 BC

Neolithic Period

Neolithic farmers first cleared the wildwood around 4000 BC, beginning the human transformation of this landscape. Archaeological evidence includes:

  • Two flint axes found on the Downs
  • Various flint tools
  • The Fairdene Road axe - a significant discovery

These early farmers chose higher ground above the tree-line where shallow soils could be worked with simple stone implements.

2300 BC - 800 BC

Bronze Age

The Bronze Age saw continued use of the Downs for agriculture and settlement. Evidence from this period includes:

  • Pits discovered during excavations
  • Pottery fragments characteristic of the period
  • Signs of continued agricultural activity

This era marked the transition from stone to bronze tools, though much of the evidence has been lost to later agricultural activity.

800 BC - 43 AD

Iron Age

Around 200 BC, the Iron Age inhabitants created a sophisticated agricultural system that's still visible today. Major features include:

  • Central droveway - A pathway running the length of the Downs for moving livestock
  • Field system - Small fields created for agriculture, with boundaries still visible as earthworks
  • Artifacts - Iron sword, pottery, protective gear, hair combs, and decorative beads

These Celtic field boundaries show how prehistoric farmers divided the land, creating one of the most significant Iron Age agricultural landscapes in the region.

43 AD - 410 AD

Roman Period

The Roman occupation brought significant change to the area. Archaeological evidence shows:

  • Evidence of Saxons already living on the Downs during the Roman era
  • Samian ware from Gaul - High-quality Roman pottery imported from France
  • Roman burials discovered at nearby Coulsdon Woods (1st century)
  • Late Roman burials at Cane Hill asylum site (2015 discoveries)

The development of the Wates estate at Stoats Nest in the late 1960s unearthed several burials with Romano-British pottery and coinage from the first century, suggesting a larger settlement pattern around the Downs.

410 AD - 1066 AD

Saxon & Medieval Period

The most visible archaeological features on Farthing Downs today are the Saxon burial mounds, dating from around 650 AD. These remarkable monuments include:

  • Three burial clusters - North, central, and south groups of mounds
  • High-status burials - Including community leaders deliberately placed on high ground to be seen for miles
  • Diverse burials - Men, women, and children interred in the mounds
  • Grave goods - Including a glass Saxon beaker found at Lion Green Road

These bowl barrows are among the most significant Anglo-Saxon funerary monuments in southern England and remain clearly visible on the ridge today.

Archaeological Summary

What the Evidence Tells Us

Apart from the artifacts and burial mounds, virtually the only hard evidence we have about these early peoples is their dead bodies - some two dozen in all discovered through archaeological digs. Where they lived, how long they lived, what they ate, and their state of health remains largely a mystery.

What is clear is that much of England at the turn of the first millennium was entering a period of profound change as immigration from the continent grew apace. The dominant Celtic culture from Gaul would soon be challenged by the arrival of the Romans - people who could read and write and had great organisational skills. Paganism would give way to Christianity, though in time the heptarchy of Anglo-Saxon tribes would force the Romans out, leaving behind the walled city of London and the period now known as the 'dark ages'.

Hope-Taylor's Groundbreaking Work

The 75th anniversary of Hope-Taylor's work in 1948 provided an opportunity to reflect not only on the light he shed on early history in this area, but also on the latest studies of Roman and Saxon history around Coulsdon, which further deepen our understanding of how significant this area was 2000 years ago.

Lasting Legacy

Hope-Taylor's work remains the cornerstone of our understanding of Farthing Downs' archaeology. His surveys and excavation records continue to inform modern archaeological investigations and site management.

Recent Discoveries: Lion Green Road

2015-2022 Excavations

Major excavations at Lion Green Road Car Park have transformed our understanding of Coulsdon's ancient history

2015 Discoveries

Bodies of two adults and a dog were discovered, together with a glass Saxon beaker. This was the first indication that the Lion Green area held significant archaeological remains.

2021 Excavations

Twelve burials were discovered on the site, revealing a much larger cemetery than previously thought. This suggested Lion Green was a significant burial ground during the Roman and Saxon periods.

2022 Final Season

A further six burials were uncovered, several containing knives - indicating these were people of some status. The Museum of London's comprehensive report runs to 54 pages with six pages of detailed illustrations.

Transforming Our Understanding

This latest archaeological work helps transform our picture and understanding of Coulsdon's inhabitants 2000 years ago, giving scope for further investigation and reappraisal of the early medieval history of Croydon and South London.

The Lion Green Road discoveries complement the burial mounds on Farthing Downs itself, suggesting a larger and more complex community than previously understood. The range of burial types, grave goods, and time periods represented show this was an important center of settlement from the Roman period through to the Saxon era.

Protecting the Past for the Future

Scheduled Ancient Monument Status

As a Scheduled Ancient Monument, Farthing Downs is protected by law. It is illegal to:

  • Use metal detectors on the site
  • Disturb the ground without permission from Historic England
  • Damage or remove archaeological features
  • Climb on burial mounds or dig near earthworks

What You Can Do

If you find something unusual:

  • Don't remove it from the ground
  • Note the exact location (use your phone's GPS)
  • Take a photo if possible
  • Report it to the City of London Rangers immediately
  • Report finds to the Portable Antiquities Scheme

The archaeological features you see today are fragile survivors of thousands of years. Please help us preserve them for future generations by treating the site with respect and following the Countryside Code.
