Wildlife

The ancient chalk grassland supports an incredible diversity of species, from tiny orchids to soaring birds of prey.

30+ Butterfly Species

Including Adonis Blue, Chalkhill Blue, Dark Green Fritillary, and the rare Silver-spotted Skipper. The Downs are a butterfly hotspot, especially in summer when wildflowers bloom.

Red Kites & Birds of Prey

Watch red kites circle overhead, along with buzzards, kestrels, and occasional hobbies. Skylarks sing above the grasslands, and green woodpeckers forage on the slopes.

Mammals

Roe deer graze at dawn and dusk. Foxes den in the scrub. Most special are the hazel dormice - rare woodland mammals that we're helping to protect through nest box schemes.

Rare Orchids

Several orchid species thrive here, including Fragrant Orchid, Common Spotted Orchid, and the scarce Pyramidal Orchid. Look but don't pick - they're all protected.

Wildflower Meadows

Spring and summer bring carpets of cowslips, harebells, rock roses, and thyme. The chalk grassland is a nationally rare habitat, protected as a Site of Special Scientific Interest.

Invertebrates

Beyond butterflies, look for grasshoppers, crickets, bees, hoverflies, and beetles. The diversity of invertebrates here is remarkable - over 1,000 species recorded.

Best Times for Wildlife

Spring (April-May) for flowers and birdsong | Summer (June-August) for butterflies | Autumn (September-October) for fungi and berries | Winter (November-March) for birds and views

Saxon Burial Mounds

Clearly visible bowl barrows on the ridge date from the 5th-7th centuries AD. These are the graves of Anglo-Saxon leaders, deliberately placed on high ground to be seen for miles around.

Celtic Field Boundaries

Faint earthworks mark ancient field systems from the Iron Age (c.800 BC - 43 AD). These boundaries show how prehistoric farmers divided the land for agriculture.

Neolithic Trackways

The Downs have been crossed by travellers for at least 6,000 years. Some paths follow routes first walked in the Stone Age, connecting settlements and trade routes.

Archaeological Finds

Flint tools, pottery fragments, and coins have been found across the site, spanning from the Mesolithic period through to Roman and medieval times. All finds are recorded and protected.

Protecting the Past

As a Scheduled Ancient Monument, it's illegal to use metal detectors or disturb the ground without permission. If you find anything unusual, please report it to the City of London Rangers - don't remove it.

The archaeological features you see today are fragile survivors of thousands of years. Please don't climb on burial mounds or dig near earthworks. Help us preserve them for future generations.

History

Ancient Landscapes

Farthing Downs and Happy Valley have been shaped by humans for over 6,000 years. Neolithic farmers first cleared the wildwood here around 4000 BC. Bronze Age burial mounds, Iron Age field systems, and Saxon graves all tell the story of communities who lived, worked, and worshipped on these hills.

The name "Farthing Downs" likely derives from "Feorthing" - an Old English term for a fourth part or division of land. "Happy Valley" is a Victorian romantic name, though the valley itself has been grazed for centuries.

City of London Corporation (Since 1883)

In 1883, the City of London Corporation purchased Farthing Downs as part of their mission to protect open spaces for public recreation. The Corporation now manages over 11,000 acres of green space across London and beyond - including Epping Forest, Hampstead Heath, and Burnham Beeches.

This ownership has protected the Downs from development for over 140 years. The land is managed by City of London Rangers who maintain access, protect wildlife, and work with volunteers like the Friends to preserve this special place.

Chalk Downland SSSI

In 1986, Farthing Downs was designated a Site of Special Scientific Interest (SSSI) for its outstanding chalk grassland habitat. This nationally rare ecosystem - created by centuries of grazing - supports species found nowhere else in the area.

SSSI status means the site is legally protected and carefully managed to maintain its special character. Cattle graze to prevent scrub encroachment. Volunteers help clear invasive species. Scientific monitoring tracks the health of rare plants and animals.
